You're in. Now make them stop scrolling.

Tip 1

Name your project — don't label it

A project title should tell someone what you built and why it matters. The form of the thing ("Website", "App", "System") tells them nothing. The subject and the problem it solves tells them everything.

Instead of this

"A Website"

Try this

International Motor Museum Digital Archive — searchable catalogue for 200+ historic vehicles
Tip 2

Write your bio for the person reading it, not the person writing it

Every student on every course in every university has "strong communication skills developed through team projects." That sentence does not distinguish you — it buries you. Write one or two sentences about what you actually built, what you find genuinely interesting, or what kind of problem you want to work on next.

Instead of this

I have developed strong communication skills through participating in team projects and understanding different points of view.

Try this

I spent my final year applying six optimisations to Grover's search algorithm and building a live archive for a museum. I like problems where the answer isn't obvious yet.
Tip 3

Say what it does, not just what you used to build it

Your tools list (Python, React, MySQL) tells a technical person you know the tools. Your project description tells everyone what you did with them. Lead with the outcome, then mention the stack.

Instead of this

Built using Python, TensorFlow, and Jupyter Notebook.

Try this

A classifier that identifies plant diseases from smartphone photos with 91% accuracy — built in Python using TensorFlow.
Tip 4

Label your links so they land

If you're linking to something other than GitHub or LinkedIn, tell the reader what they'll find there. “Website” could be anything. “Live demo — trolleybus museum archive” tells them exactly what to click and why.

The employers coming on 19th June are not marking your degree. They are looking for someone they want to work with. Your profile is your side of that conversation starting before you're in the room.

Give it fifteen minutes. It will be worth it.
